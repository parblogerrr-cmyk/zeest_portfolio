Journal Entry 9

Politeness Has a Rank

One thing I am very clear about politeness is that it is not evenly distributed, it follows power. Who gets to be direct, who has to soften their words, and who must stay silent often depends less on personality and more on position.

We discussed how power influences politeness strategies and I immediately thought of classrooms, offices, and even family settings in Pakistan. A teacher can say, “Submit this today,” without sounding rude but a student saying the same would be labelled disrespectful. The difference isn’t linguistic correctness, it’s authority. Those with power can afford to threaten face because their own face is already protected.

This imbalance shows up strongly in workplaces. Junior employees often wrap requests in layers of politeness—“If it’s not too much trouble…” or “Whenever you find time…”—even when the task is urgent. Seniors, however, can issue commands with minimal softening. Politeness here becomes a survival strategy, not a choice.

Gender complicates this further. In Pakistani society, women are often expected to remain polite regardless of their position. A woman being direct risks being labelled “rude” or “arrogant,” while the same tone in a man is interpreted as confidence. The discourse of politeness ends up reinforcing existing power structures instead of simply managing interaction.

What I realized is that we rarely question why respect flows upward more than downward. This lecture made me see politeness not as moral goodness, but as a social tool, one that can maintain harmony, yes, but also quietly preserve inequality.

So, if politeness always bends toward power, is it really about kindness or about control?
