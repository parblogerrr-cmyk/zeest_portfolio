Journal Entry 2

Becoming Someone Through Words

I used to think identity was something fixed, something we simply are. This course made me realize how much of it is actually something we are told, repeatedly, through discourse. The way the world is described to us slowly becomes the way we see ourselves in it.

We discussed how discourse constructs worldviews and identities, and once that idea settled in, it became impossible for me to ignore. For instance, in Pakistan, the repeated discourse of being a “developing nation” or a “fragile democracy” doesn’t just describe reality, it shapes expectations. It subtly lowers the bar. When corruption or poor governance is framed as “normal for a country like ours,” that discourse produces resignation rather than resistance. I’ve caught myself accepting things simply because I’ve heard them described that way for years.

On an international level, the discourse around Muslims after 9/11 is a striking example. Words like terror suspect, radicalized, or moderate Muslim didn’t just label actions; they created our identities. Entire communities were pushed into defensive positions, constantly having to prove harmlessness. That discourse didn’t emerge naturally rather it was produced, repeated, and normalized through global media.

What disturbed me most was realizing how easily discourse divides people into categories: “us” and “them,” “civilized” and “backward,” “empowered” and “oppressed.” Even gender identities are shaped this way. When leadership is consistently described in masculine terms, women who step into those roles are seen as exceptions rather than norms.

This lecture forced me to ask an uncomfortable question: how much of who I think I am is actually my own and how much has been quietly written for me through language?
