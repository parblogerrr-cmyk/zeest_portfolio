Journal Entry 6

When Words Decide What Feels “Normal”

At some point, I stopped asking why certain things felt normal and started asking who taught me they were. This week I felt it impossible to ignore how deeply discourse shapes our perceptions, so deeply that constructed realities begin to feel natural, unquestionable, and permanent.

We discussed how reality itself is created through discourse, and gender was one of the clearest examples. From childhood, we are surrounded by language that quietly assigns roles. Nursery rhymes like “Daddy goes to office, Mommy stays at home” may sound harmless, but they repeatedly link masculinity with productivity and femininity with care work. Long before children understand social theory, they’ve already absorbed a worldview.

Brand culture plays a similar role. Cleaning products marketed to women and tech products marketed to men reinforce ideas about who belongs where. Even jokes contribute. Casual remarks like “women can’t park” or “men don’t cry” are often dismissed as humor, yet they repeatedly frame behavior as natural rather than learned.

What unsettled me most was realizing how early and subtly this conditioning begins. Discourse doesn’t shout; it whispers, repeats, and settles in. This lecture made me more alert to the language around me and more responsible for the language I use. If words can build realities, then choosing them carelessly isn’t harmless at all.
