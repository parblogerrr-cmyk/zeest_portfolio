New Campus New Crisis

The first week at the new campus felt less like stepping into a place of learning and more like walking into a hospital ward. The walls, painted in shades that do nothing but dull the eye, stood in stiff silence. The building itself, just one lone block dropped in the middle of a restless construction sit, seemed to mock the very idea of a “campus.” There is nothing sprawling, nothing welcoming, nothing that breathes of growth. Instead, it feels confined, a structure standing awkwardly, as if unsure of its own purpose.

And yet, within this uninspiring shell, there are surprises. The classrooms, to my relief, are airy and washed with light. Sunlight spills through wide windows, softening the sterile atmosphere, as if reminding me that even in lifeless walls, warmth can find its way. I sit in these rooms and notice how space and light can change everything the same lecture, the same faces, but somehow made gentler by the openness.

Still, what I am most thankful for is not the light or the architecture, but the people. The place has changed, but the familiar faces remain. In their laughter, in their easy presence beside me, I find the texture of home. Their company cushions the starkness of this new space. The building may fail to inspire, but the people around me keep me grounded, keep me belonging.

So my first week here is a paradox, an ugly building, yet bright rooms; a barren site, yet a circle of warmth. Perhaps that is the lesson this campus is already teaching me: place matters less than the people who fill it.
