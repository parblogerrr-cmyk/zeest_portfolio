Introduction to Discourse

I walked into the first lecture thinking language was simply a tool we use to express thoughts. I walked out realizing it’s also a tool that shapes them. That shift in perspective is what stayed with me the most. Discourse, as we discussed, isn’t just about sentences or grammar, it’s about how language operates within real-life situations, social structures, and power relations.

What struck me was the idea that context is everything. I kept thinking about how in Pakistan, the phrase “boys will be boys” is treated like a harmless joke. On the surface, it’s just a casual line. But through a discourse lens, it normalizes male misbehavior, excuses harassment, and quietly blames girls for “not being careful enough.” The same words in a different social context say, in a women’s rights rally, would be quoted with sarcasm to expose patriarchy. Same sentence, totally different function and meaning because the context shifts.

The lecture also challenged the comforting belief that language is neutral. It really isn’t. Even everyday phrases carry ideology. In Pakistan, this becomes very visible in political and media discourse. A statement like “national interest” sounds neutral and harmless, but it often hides very specific agendas. When politicians use this phrase, it usually discourages questioning almost as if disagreeing becomes unpatriotic. That made me realize how cleverly discourse can silence opposition without explicitly doing so.

We also talked about the functions of discourse, how it informs, persuades, and sometimes controls. Looking around, it’s hard not to notice how often language is used to normalize inequality or justify authority. This lecture made me more alert, more suspicious in a healthy way. It left me asking: when I hear something repeatedly, am I accepting reality or just accepting a well-packaged discourse?
