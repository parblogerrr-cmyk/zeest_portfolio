Speaking Well Is Not the Same as Speaking Right

This week I was challenged something I had always assumed that, being fluent meant being effective. I’ve seen people spending years working on vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation, but communicative competence made me realize that accuracy alone doesn’t guarantee successful communication.

What stood out was how competence operates on multiple levels at once. Grammatical knowledge matters, of course, but it’s only one part of the picture. Knowing how to structure a sentence doesn’t tell you whether it should be said, how directly, or in what tone. I started noticing this gap in everyday academic and professional interactions.

For example, in formal emails, many students (including myself at times) write sentences that are grammatically perfect but pragmatically off. A line like “Send me the document today” isn’t incorrect, yet it ignores sociolinguistic expectations of politeness and hierarchy. Adding mitigation doesn’t weaken the message; it strengthens its acceptability within that discourse community.

The lectures also highlighted discourse competence, the ability to maintain coherence and relevance. I realized how often communication fails not because of wrong words, but because ideas aren’t framed in ways listeners expect. Strategic competence felt especially practical, it’s the ability to repair misunderstandings, rephrase, or adapt when communication breaks down (and most people lack this one). It made me rethink that effective communication isn’t about showing how much language you know rather it’s about knowing how to fit language into real situations.
