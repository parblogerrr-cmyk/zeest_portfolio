Journal Entry 13

Smiling for the Invisible Camera

The idea of the Panopticon sounded dramatic at first, almost like something from a dystopian novel. But the more we discussed discursive surveillance, the more I realized: we’re already living in it, just with better lighting and Wi-Fi. No tower, no guard just the feeling that someone might be watching, so you behave accordingly.

In the Pakistani context, this feels painfully familiar. From CCTV cameras announced loudly on signboards to moral surveillance on social media, discipline is often enforced without direct force. You don’t need to be told what’s “acceptable”, discourse does that work for you. A tweet, a comment section, or a viral clip is enough to correct behavior. Fear isn’t punishment, it’s public judgment.

What fascinates me is how surveillance becomes internal. People censor themselves before anyone else does. Women adjust clothing choices, students monitor opinions, journalists choose words carefully, all because discourse has trained us to anticipate consequences. Even phrases like “log kya kahenge” function as a local Panopticon: no watcher visible, but control fully active.

There’s something darkly funny about it too. We joke about being “under watch” while willingly posting our lives online. The Panopticon no longer needs walls; it runs on likes, outrage, and silence.

This lecture made me realize that power today doesn’t always shout. Sometimes, it just watches and lets discourse do the rest.
