When Words Don’t Mean What They Say

This lecture was surprisingly fun, but also quietly unsettling. It started with a simple dialogue “Could you pass the salt?” and somehow ended with me questioning how often we misunderstand each other without even realizing it. The sentence itself is polite, grammatically correct, and harmless. Yet, as our teacher showed, its form asks about ability, while its function is a request. When B answered “Yes” and continued eating, it felt absurd but also familiar.

What this example made clear is that meaning doesn’t live in grammar alone. We rely heavily on shared social understanding to interpret what people intend, not just what they say. Discourse works because we usually cooperate in filling these gaps. When that cooperation fails, communication collapses in the most awkward ways.

I began thinking about how often this happens in real life, especially in institutional or formal settings. For instance, when a university administration says, “Students are advised to maintain discipline,” it looks like friendly guidance on the surface. But functionally, it often operates as a warning. The form sounds optional; the function is anything but.

It made me more aware of how politeness, indirectness, and convention shape communication. We rarely speak directly, yet we somehow expect others to understand us perfectly. That expectation itself is fascinating. It made me realize that miscommunication isn’t always about language deficiency, it’s about mismatched assumptions.

What interested me most was how fragile understanding actually is. If meaning depends so much on shared norms, what happens when those norms differ? How much do we assume rather than understand when we listen?
