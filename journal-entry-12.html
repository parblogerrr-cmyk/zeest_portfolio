Journal Entry 12

Seeing What Others Walk Past

Before this lecture, I never really looked at images, I just consumed them. Magazine covers, billboards, logos, Instagram ads, all blended into the background of daily life. But learning about Multimodal Discourse Analysis completely disrupted that comfort. Now, I can’t unsee the strategy behind everything visual.

What surprised me most is how intentional every detail is. A close-up shot creates intimacy; a distant shot creates authority or mystery. A low camera angle makes a person look powerful, while a high angle quietly diminishes them. Lighting decides whether someone appears trustworthy or threatening. Colors aren’t chosen for beauty alone, red demands attention, black signals seriousness, pastels soften meaning. Even font size and spacing shout hierarchy: this matters more than that.

A layperson might say, “It’s just a billboard.” But we know better. We know that language, image, layout, and design work together to guide interpretation without asking permission. That’s what makes it powerful and slightly unsettling.

At the same time, I’d be lying if I said it doesn’t make me feel cool. Being able to decode visuals feels like having backstage access to meaning-making. This lecture didn’t just teach me analysis; it gave me visual literacy. I don’t just see anymore, I read images, and they speak loudly.
